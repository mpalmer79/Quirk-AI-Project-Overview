The Quirk AI Workplace Verification system integrates facial recognition technology with your existing biometric time clock to ensure employees are present in appropriate areas during work hours. It is designed specifically for automotive dealership operations with a compliance-first architecture. The camera network is connected to a central software platform that uses artificial intelligence to identify individuals in the video feed by comparing their facial features to a database of employee photos. Beyond simply verifying identities at a time clock, these systems can enable continuous tracking of employee movements within a building, monitor which areas employees enter, and record time spent at specific workstations or common areas.

Is it possible?

Facial recognition software that integrates with Paycom. Check employee activity while they are in the workplace against their timecard. Are they still on the premises while clocked in? Let’s create an AI-powered solution for this.

While no current software automatically and continuously checks employee activity against time cards using facial recognition with Paycom, it is technologically possible to build such a system. There are third-party vendors that offer AI-powered facial recognition time clocks and monitoring, and these can be integrated with HR platforms like Paycom.

How an AI-powered system for continuous monitoring would work

A theoretical system with these capabilities would be more invasive than a simple biometric time clock and would need to be built with special consideration for privacy and legality.

  • Surveillance hardware: Install AI-powered camera systems not just at entrances and exits, but throughout the workplace floor.
  • Facial tracking: Continuously match faces with a database of pre-registered templates to track movements in real time.
  • Premise verification: Confirm an employee is still on the premises while clocked in; trigger an alert if they leave a designated area or are lost by the system.
  • Activity cross-reference: Compare AI tracking data with timecard information in Paycom (likely via API) to flag discrepancies such as off-premises while clocked in.

Legal and ethical concerns

Building a system for continuous tracking raises far greater legal and ethical issues than a time clock.

  • Consent and notice: Several states (e.g., Illinois, Texas, Washington) regulate the collection of biometric information; Illinois’s BIPA is especially strict and requires prior written notice and consent.
  • Increased litigation risk: Potential for large class-action lawsuits over biometric privacy violations, particularly in jurisdictions like Illinois.
  • Privacy invasion and morale: Continuous facial recognition monitoring may erode trust and harm morale; it can have a chilling effect on the workforce.
  • Bias and discrimination: Facial recognition algorithms can exhibit significant biases against people of color and women, leading to discriminatory outcomes and legal exposure.
  • Mission creep: Technology might be expanded to track other metrics (e.g., engagement, time on task) without additional consent.

Existing Paycom alternatives

Less intrusive, still robust time-tracking options that don’t rely on continuous facial recognition monitoring:

  • Microfence: Bluetooth beacons in workplace zones; employees can clock in/out in the Paycom app only within range, confirming on-site presence.
  • Geofencing: GPS-based virtual boundary limits clock in/out to a designated area via the Paycom mobile app.
  • App-based biometrics: Fingerprint or Face ID secures the app itself (separate from workplace monitoring).
